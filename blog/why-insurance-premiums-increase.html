Why Do Insurance Premiums Go Up? Complete NZ Guide

Understanding the factors that affect your insurance costs and practical strategies for managing premium increases

One of the most common questions I hear from clients is: "Why has my insurance premium gone up so much?" Whether it's life insurance, income protection, or health cover, rising premiums are frustrating. But understanding what drives these increases can help you make smarter decisions about your coverage and costs.

The Main Factors Driving Insurance Premium Increases

1. Age and Health Changes

The most significant factor affecting your insurance cost is your age. Insurance companies price risk, and as you get older, the statistical likelihood of claiming increases. This is why premiums for life and income protection insurance rise year after year.

Health changes also impact your premiums significantly. If you develop a medical condition, receive a diagnosis, or experience lifestyle changes, your insurer may reassess your risk profile and increase your premium. This is why it's crucial to review your coverage regularly and be transparent with your insurer about health changes.

2. Claims History

If you've made a claim on your policy, your insurer will often increase your premium when you renew. This is because you've now been identified as a higher-risk customer. In some cases, multiple claims can lead to significant premium increases or even policy cancellation.

Different claim types are weighted differently. A minor claim may result in a smaller increase, while serious or multiple claims can substantially increase your costs.

3. Market Conditions and Claims Data

Insurance companies use actuarial data to assess overall risk. When industry-wide claims increase—whether due to higher mortality rates, more disability claims, or increased healthcare costs—insurers raise premiums across the board to maintain profitability.

In New Zealand, we've seen premium increases driven by factors like rising healthcare costs, increased longevity (living longer = higher claim costs), and changes in claim patterns. Economic conditions also play a role: during difficult economic times, insurers see more claims and adjust pricing accordingly.

4. Inflation and Cost Increases

Medical expenses, medication costs, and treatment options continue to rise. When your income protection or health insurance benefit amount stays the same, but actual costs increase, your insurer adjusts premiums to reflect these economic realities.

5. Increased Coverage or Benefit Changes

Sometimes premium increases occur because you've changed your coverage—increasing your benefit amount, extending the claim period, or adding additional coverage types. Make sure you understand what changes triggered any premium adjustment.

How Much Should Premiums Increase?

Annual premium increases of 3-5% are relatively normal and roughly align with inflation. However, increases above 10% should prompt you to investigate further. If you're experiencing significant increases without changes to your health or coverage, it's worth discussing with your adviser to understand why.

How to Manage Rising Insurance Costs

Review Your Coverage Regularly

Annual reviews help you understand exactly what you're paying for and whether it still matches your needs. As your circumstances change—career advancement, family growth, property ownership—your coverage needs evolve too.

Compare Policies and Insurers

Insurance pricing varies significantly between providers. Every few years, it's worth getting quotes from different insurers to see if better rates are available. However, make sure any new policy is appropriate for your health profile—changing insurers when your health has deteriorated may not be beneficial.

Increase Excess/Deductible

If you're comfortable taking on more financial risk yourself, increasing your policy excess can reduce your premium. This works well if you don't need to claim frequently, but ensure you have funds available to cover a larger excess if needed.

Adjust Benefit Amounts

Reducing your benefit amount will lower your premium, though this should only be done if your financial circumstances have genuinely changed. The goal is to maintain adequate coverage while managing costs.

Bundle Policies

Some insurers offer discounts when you combine multiple policies—life insurance, income protection, and health cover. Bundling can result in meaningful savings.

Maintain Good Health

While you can't stop aging, maintaining good health helps minimize additional premium increases from health-related risk assessments. Regular exercise, not smoking, and managing chronic conditions can help.

What About Switching Insurers?

If your current insurer's premiums have become uncompetitive, switching is an option. However, consider these factors: New insurers may perform full health underwriting (potentially resulting in exclusions if your health has changed), you'll lose any loyalty benefits, and the switching process takes time.

It's usually worth investigating if you're experiencing significant premium increases, but get professional advice to ensure you maintain adequate coverage during any transition.

The Bottom Line

Insurance premium increases are a normal part of insurance ownership, but they don't have to catch you off guard. By understanding what drives these increases, reviewing your coverage regularly, and exploring your options, you can make confident decisions about your insurance costs.
